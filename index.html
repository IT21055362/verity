Litreature Review

Social bot detection, a crucial area of research, refers to the identification and analysis of these bots on social media platforms. As social bots continue to evolve, they are becoming increasingly difficult to detect. Detecting social bots is crucial for maintaining the integrity and reliability of social media platforms. Due to the evolving nature of these bots, social bot detection remains a widely researched area.

Recent advancements have seen a shift towards machine learning (ML) and deep learning (DL) techniques. A systematic review [3] highlighted that DL methods, particularly those utilizing convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and recurrent neural networks (RNNs), have shown significant promise in distinguishing between human and bot accounts due to their ability to learn complex patterns in data. However, the authors also identify gaps and challenges in current DL approaches, such as the need for better feature representation and handling the multilingual nature of social media content.

Another systematic review on social media bot detection methods, [4] highlighted that machine learning techniques, especially supervised learning, emerged as the predominant approach. Features used for detection can be broadly categorized into profile information, post content, posting behaviour, and network structure. While graph-based and crowdsourcing approaches offer unique insights, they face scalability issues. Unsupervised learning shows promise in identifying coordinated bot activities. Notably, there is an increasing focus on content-based features, especially those leveraging natural language processing, as these can capture subtle linguistic patterns characteristic of bot behaviour. This multi-faceted approach reflects the complex and evolving nature of social media bots, necessitating diverse and sophisticated detection strategies.

For instance, Kumar et al.[5] proposed a novel approach for detecting Twitter bots using only tweet content. They developed an ensemble model combining TextCNN (a convolutional neural network for text classification) and LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory) with BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers) embeddings, achieving 97% accuracy and outperforming previous methods. To address class imbalance, they introduced a Language Model Oversampling Technique (LMOTE) that generated more semantically meaningful synthetic bot tweets compared to traditional methods. The ensemble architecture effectively captured both n-gram relationships and sequential patterns in the text. Overall, the study demonstrated that advanced NLP techniques and data augmentation can significantly improve automated social media bot detection using textual features alone, without relying on account metadata or network information.

Similarly, Wei and Nguyen [6] introduced BOTLE, a novel Twitter bot detection model utilizing bidirectional lightweight gated recurrent units (BiLGRU) and linguistic embeddings. This approach achieves comparable or superior performance to state-of- the-art models without relying on handcrafted features or prior user information. The study highlights the potential of text-based bot detection methods in social media analysis. However, the model's reliance solely on tweet content may limit its ability to detect sophisticated bots that mimic human language patterns. Additionally, the study does not address the model's adaptability to evolving bot behaviours or its performance on more recent datasets, which could be potential limitations in real-world applications.

Furthermore, Martín-Gutiérrez et al. [7] introduced a multilingual approach for detecting Twitter bots using transformers and deep learning. The researchers developed Bot-DenseNet, a model that combined metadata features with text-based features encoded using language models like BERT and RoBERTa, addressing language constraints of previous methods. Their model achieved a high F1-score of 0.9715 using RoBERTa embeddings, outperforming other input representations tested. The researchers faced with data imbalance, requiring careful handling of their dataset which contained more human than bot accounts. The evolving nature of bot behaviour posed another significant challenge, necessitating a model that could generalize well to potentially unseen tactics. Balancing feature selection between metadata and text-based features, while also managing model complexity and avoiding overfitting, were crucial considerations.

The detection of Twitter bots in specific contexts, such as the Sri Lankan political landscape, further highlights the importance of adaptable bot detection systems. As highlighted by the study of the 2015 presidential election, [8] bots like Siripalabot inadvertently amplified alternative voices in a media environment where government control was a concern. This created a new mediated space for democratic dialogue, particularly important in a country with growing internet and social media use.

In conclusion, the above studies demonstrate significant advancements in social bot detection, particularly in leveraging deep learning techniques and linguistic features. However, challenges remain in adapting to evolving bot behaviours, handling multilingual content, and balancing between textual and non-textual features. Our research aims to address these gaps by developing a more comprehensive model that efficiently combines various feature types while maintaining high detection accuracy.
