Litreature Review

In recent years, the emergence and rapid advancement of deepfake technology have sparked significant concern across various sectors of society. Deepfake images, a subset of synthetic media, are artificially created or manipulated visual content generated through sophisticated artificial intelligence and machine learning techniques, particularly deep learning and generative adversarial networks (GANs). These technologies have enabled the production of highly realistic fake images that can be nearly indistinguishable from authentic photographs to the human eye. The creation of deepfake images typically involves training AI models on large datasets of real images, allowing the system to learn and replicate patterns, textures, and features with remarkable accuracy. This process can be used to generate entirely new, yet convincingly realistic images of people who don't exist, or to manipulate existing images by swapping faces, altering expressions, or changing various elements within the picture. While deepfake technology has some positive applications in fields such as entertainment, art, and education, its potential for misuse has raised alarming concerns among experts, policymakers, and the general public. The implications of widespread deepfake image creation and distribution are far-reaching and multifaceted. The most pressing concern is the potential for deepfakes to be used as a tool for spreading false information. In an era where visual content plays a crucial role in shaping public opinion, the ability to create convincing fake images of events that never occurred or manipulate existing images to change their context poses a significant threat to informed decision-making and democratic processes. Deepfake technology enables the creation of synthetic images featuring real individuals without their knowledge or consent. This capability has been particularly problematic in the realm of non-consensual pornography, where victims' faces can be superimposed onto explicit content, leading to severe emotional distress, reputational damage, and potential for blackmail. The rise of deepfake images presents complex legal and ethical questions regarding accountability, intellectual property, and the boundaries of free speech. Existing laws and ethical frameworks may be ill-equipped to address the unique challenges posed by this technology. Research Gap

The rapid advancement of artificial intelligence and machine learning technologies has led to the proliferation of deepfake images, presenting significant challenges to digital media authenticity and security. While several studies have explored deepfake detection techniques, there remains a critical gap in developing comprehensive, context- specific solutions, particularly for regions like Sri Lanka. This research aims to address these gaps by proposing a hybrid model that combines existing deepfake image detection techniques with convolutional neural networks (CNN) and transfer learning methods. Despite the commendable efforts that have been made to tackle the challenges of deepfake image detection, a critical research gap persists, impeding the development of comprehensive solutions. This gap can be defined by a set of key challenges that remain unaddressed within the current field of deepfake image detection, which are listed below: 1. Context-Specific Models: Current deepfake detection models often lack adaptability to specific cultural and regional contexts. In the case of Sri Lanka, there is a notable absence of models tailored to the unique digital landscape, linguistic nuances, and cultural specificities of the region. 2. Hybrid Model Optimization: While some research has explored hybrid approaches, there is a lack of models that effectively combine multiple detection techniques in a way that maximizes their collective strengths. The optimization of hybrid models for deepfake detection remains an underexplored area. 3. Transfer Learning: The application of transfer learning in deepfake detection, particularly for adapting models to new contexts or limited datasets, is not fully realized. There is a need for more efficient transfer learning methods that can quickly adapt pre-trained models to specific regional contexts like Sri Lanka. 4. Efficient Detection Capabilities: Many existing models focus on post-hoc analysis rather than real-time detection. There is a pressing need for solutions that can identify deepfakes in real-time, especially given the rapid spread of manipulated media on social platforms. To address the identified research gaps and challenges in deepfake image detection, this research component proposes the development of an innovative hybrid deepfake image detection model. This model aims to combine the strengths of existing detection methods with advanced deep learning techniques. The proposed solution centers around the creation of a hybrid architecture that integrates established deepfake detection methods, such as the DenseNet121 model, with custom convolutional neural networks (CNNs). DenseNet121 has shown promising results in image classification tasks due to its dense connectivity pattern, which allows for more efficient feature reuse and improved gradient flow. By incorporating this proven architecture, the model leverages its ability to extract features from images. The integration of custom CNNs alongside DenseNet121 allows for the development of specialized layers tailored to the unique characteristics of deepfake images. These custom CNN layers can be designed to focus on specific artifacts or inconsistencies commonly found in manipulated images, such as unnatural blending boundaries, inconsistent lighting, or anomalies in facial features. This hybrid approach aims to create a novel and robust detection system capable of identifying a wider range of deepfake techniques. A key innovation in this proposed model is the application of transfer learning techniques to fine-tune the hybrid architecture for specific contexts. The model development process will occur in two distinct phases. Initially, the hybrid model will be trained on a large, diverse dataset representing a global context. This dataset will include a wide variety of deepfake images from different sources. The goal of this phase is to develop a strong foundational model capable of detecting deepfakes. After achieving a high level of accuracy in the global context, the model will undergo a fine-tuning phase using a smaller, dataset specific to the Sri Lankan context. This dataset will include images featuring Sri Lankan faces and the fine-tuning process will adapt the model's learned features related to the Sri Lankan facial characteristics.

Litreature Review

Social bot detection, a crucial area of research, refers to the identification and analysis of these bots on social media platforms. As social bots continue to evolve, they are becoming increasingly difficult to detect. Detecting social bots is crucial for maintaining the integrity and reliability of social media platforms. Due to the evolving nature of these bots, social bot detection remains a widely researched area.

Recent advancements have seen a shift towards machine learning (ML) and deep learning (DL) techniques. A systematic review [3] highlighted that DL methods, particularly those utilizing convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and recurrent neural networks (RNNs), have shown significant promise in distinguishing between human and bot accounts due to their ability to learn complex patterns in data. However, the authors also identify gaps and challenges in current DL approaches, such as the need for better feature representation and handling the multilingual nature of social media content.

Another systematic review on social media bot detection methods, [4] highlighted that machine learning techniques, especially supervised learning, emerged as the predominant approach. Features used for detection can be broadly categorized into profile information, post content, posting behaviour, and network structure. While graph-based and crowdsourcing approaches offer unique insights, they face scalability issues. Unsupervised learning shows promise in identifying coordinated bot activities. Notably, there is an increasing focus on content-based features, especially those leveraging natural language processing, as these can capture subtle linguistic patterns characteristic of bot behaviour. This multi-faceted approach reflects the complex and evolving nature of social media bots, necessitating diverse and sophisticated detection strategies.

For instance, Kumar et al.[5] proposed a novel approach for detecting Twitter bots using only tweet content. They developed an ensemble model combining TextCNN (a convolutional neural network for text classification) and LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory) with BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers) embeddings, achieving 97% accuracy and outperforming previous methods. To address class imbalance, they introduced a Language Model Oversampling Technique (LMOTE) that generated more semantically meaningful synthetic bot tweets compared to traditional methods. The ensemble architecture effectively captured both n-gram relationships and sequential patterns in the text. Overall, the study demonstrated that advanced NLP techniques and data augmentation can significantly improve automated social media bot detection using textual features alone, without relying on account metadata or network information.

Similarly, Wei and Nguyen [6] introduced BOTLE, a novel Twitter bot detection model utilizing bidirectional lightweight gated recurrent units (BiLGRU) and linguistic embeddings. This approach achieves comparable or superior performance to state-of- the-art models without relying on handcrafted features or prior user information. The study highlights the potential of text-based bot detection methods in social media analysis. However, the model's reliance solely on tweet content may limit its ability to detect sophisticated bots that mimic human language patterns. Additionally, the study does not address the model's adaptability to evolving bot behaviours or its performance on more recent datasets, which could be potential limitations in real-world applications.

Furthermore, Martín-Gutiérrez et al. [7] introduced a multilingual approach for detecting Twitter bots using transformers and deep learning. The researchers developed Bot-DenseNet, a model that combined metadata features with text-based features encoded using language models like BERT and RoBERTa, addressing language constraints of previous methods. Their model achieved a high F1-score of 0.9715 using RoBERTa embeddings, outperforming other input representations tested. The researchers faced with data imbalance, requiring careful handling of their dataset which contained more human than bot accounts. The evolving nature of bot behaviour posed another significant challenge, necessitating a model that could generalize well to potentially unseen tactics. Balancing feature selection between metadata and text-based features, while also managing model complexity and avoiding overfitting, were crucial considerations.

The detection of Twitter bots in specific contexts, such as the Sri Lankan political landscape, further highlights the importance of adaptable bot detection systems. As highlighted by the study of the 2015 presidential election, [8] bots like Siripalabot inadvertently amplified alternative voices in a media environment where government control was a concern. This created a new mediated space for democratic dialogue, particularly important in a country with growing internet and social media use.

In conclusion, the above studies demonstrate significant advancements in social bot detection, particularly in leveraging deep learning techniques and linguistic features. However, challenges remain in adapting to evolving bot behaviours, handling multilingual content, and balancing between textual and non-textual features. Our research aims to address these gaps by developing a more comprehensive model that efficiently combines various feature types while maintaining high detection accuracy.

Research Gap

While recent studies have made significant progress in social bot detection using advanced machine learning techniques and linguistic features, there remains a lack of comprehensive models that effectively combine textual and non-textual features while adapting to evolving bot behaviours across different cultural and linguistic contexts. Current bot detection methods often rely heavily on account metadata or network analysis, which may not capture the nuanced language patterns and behaviours specific to Sri Lankan social media users. There is a notable lack of research that combines language pattern analysis with deep learning techniques, specifically tailored to the Sri Lankan context. While some studies have explored the use of language models and transformers for multilingual bot detection, they have not adequately addressed the specific challenges posed by Sri Lankan languages and social media behaviours. Additionally, the integration of Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) in bot detection models, which could potentially capture complex temporal patterns in user behaviour and language use, has not been sufficiently explored in the context of Sri Lankan social media. This research aims to address these gaps by: 1. Developing a bot detection model that incorporates both language pattern analysis and user behavior metrics, specifically tailored to the Sri Lankan context. 2. Utilizing RNNs to capture temporal patterns in language use and user behavior, potentially improving detection accuracy for sophisticated bots. 3. Creating a model that can be initially trained on a global dataset and then fine tuned with a smaller, localized Sri Lankan dataset, addressing the scarcity of large-scale, region-specific training data. 4. Investigating the effectiveness of this approach in detecting bots that use local languages and dialects, which may not be adequately captured by existing global models. 10 By addressing these gaps, this research aims to contribute to more effective bot detection methods, particularly valuable for maintaining the integrity of online discourse in Sri Lanka's social media landscape.

More recently, hybrid deep learning architectures that combine convolutional neural networks (CNN) and recurrent neural networks (RNN) have emerged as a promising approach, achieving over 80% accuracy in detecting fake news on tweets. Several independent variables were analyzed to study their impact on the system's accuracy, including number of training cycles, data diversity, and vector size. The results showed that increasing the number of training epochs beyond an optimal point led to overfitting and stagnation in validation accuracy. Data diversity, in terms of the subject matter and sources of the news articles in the training data, had a significant effect on the system's generalization ability when tested on a separate dataset. Vector size also influenced accuracy, with larger vector sizes negatively impacting performance, especially when training data was limited. Overall, the proposed system achieved high accuracy of over 94% when trained on a diverse dataset and tested on an independent test set, highlighting the efficacy of combining LSTM and Word2Vec for automated fake news detection. However, the system performed poorly (63% accuracy) when trained on a narrowly focused dataset lacking diversity, underscoring the importance of representative and varied training data. A comprehensive review of machine learning models adopted for fake news detection by Gowthami et al [4] reports the performance of the best models in terms of accuracy, F1- score, precision and recall. The traditional algorithms included Random Forest, Naive Bayes, Decision Trees, SVM (Support Vector Machines), and Logistic Regression. Deep learning techniques also provided intelligent insights. They included CNN and LSTM such as, Bi-LSTM, C-LSTM Hierarchical Attention Networks, or their hybrid model CNN-LSTM. Among these, the Bi-LSTM and C-LSTM models achieved the highest accuracy of 95% on a combined corpus for fake news detection. The paper highlights the superiority of deep learning techniques, especially LSTM-based architectures, in capturing the complex patterns and linguistic nuances present in fake news data. However, the performance of models is heavily influenced by the diversity and representativeness of the training data used. 5 Mollah et al [5] presents a cutting-edge approach to fake news detection by synergistically combining the powerful RoBERTa language model with a meticulously crafted deep neural network architecture. Recognizing the limitations of previous methods, the authors leverage RoBERTa's contextual word embeddings to capture the nuanced linguistic patterns indicative of misinformation. These embeddings are then fed into a customized deep neural network designed explicitly for the fake news detection task. The network's architecture incorporates attention mechanisms, dropout layers for regularization, and dense layers for dimensionality reduction, culminating in a binary classification output. Comprehensive experiments on the well-curated WELFake dataset demonstrate the remarkable efficacy of this approach, achieving an exceptional accuracy of 99.76% and outperforming prior state-of-the-art models by a substantial margin. The authors attribute this outstanding performance to the synergy between RoBERTa's language representation capabilities and the tailored deep learning architecture, underscoring the potential of advanced language models and neural networks in combating the pernicious spread of fake news. Research Gap

When designing a system to detect misinformation, several critical factors must be considered, particularly in the realm of political misinformation on social media platforms like Twitter. While extensive research has been conducted on computational approaches to misinformation detection, a significant gap remains in effectively addressing the unique challenges posed by political misinformation, especially in the context of Twitter. This section will discuss the intricacies of the proposed system and compare it with existing approaches, highlighting how our system aims to bridge this 5 gap. Existing Twitter-based misinformation detection methods often fall short in several key areas. A notable limitation is their inadequate handling of the nuanced language typically used in political propaganda. Political misinformation often involves sophisticated rhetoric, subtle insinuations, and context-specific references, which can easily bypass traditional detection algorithms. Furthermore, many of these systems overly rely on external sources that are not always verified, which compromises the accuracy and reliability of the detection process. Additionally, these systems rarely incorporate mechanisms to validate claims against well-established and credible references, resulting in a higher likelihood of false positives or missed detections. In the specific context of Sri Lanka, research on political misinformation detection is notably sparse. Few studies have been conducted, leaving a significant void in this critical area. Among the limited research, Lalinda et al. developed a hybrid approach that combines content-based analysis with social network-based analysis to detect fake news in Sinhala text. This approach, while innovative, requires substantial preprocessing steps, such as feature extraction and network analysis, to function effectively. Another noteworthy study by Rahul et al. proposed a technique based on an autoencoder model to detect fake news. This method is particularly interesting as it captures patterns in data without requiring a labeled dataset, which is often a limitation in supervised learning models. However, like Lalinda et al.'s approach, it also demands significant preprocessing, which can be resource-intensive and time-consuming. Our proposed system seeks to overcome these limitations by adopting an end-to-end Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) model, which eliminates the need for extensive feature extraction and preprocessing. This model will directly process raw text data, learning to identify patterns and nuances in the language used in political misinformation on Twitter. By utilizing an RNN, our system can more effectively capture the temporal dependencies and context-specific language patterns that are often present in political propaganda. This approach not only streamlines the detection process but also enhances the system's ability to adapt to the evolving nature of misinformation. 6 A key innovation of our proposed system is the incorporation of external validation mechanisms from credible news sources. This feature will serve as a critical layer of verification, helping to ensure that the detected content is cross-referenced with trustworthy information. This step is particularly important in the context of political misinformation, where the stakes are high, and the consequences of false positives can be significant. Moreover, our system will be specifically fine-tuned to address the local context of Sri Lanka. Given the linguistic and cultural specificities of the region, we will create a small, dedicated dataset by collecting tweets in the Sinhala language. This dataset will be instrumental in training and optimizing our model, ensuring that it is not only effective in general scenarios but also tailored to the unique characteristics of the Sri Lankan digital landscape. In summary, our project aims to fill the existing gaps in political misinformation detection on Twitter by developing a novel system that is both context-aware and technically advanced. By addressing the shortcomings of prior approaches and introducing new features such as end-to-end RNN processing and external validation, our system represents a significant advancement in the field. This innovation is expected to greatly enhance the accuracy and reliability of political misinformation detection, particularly in challenging environments like Twitter.
