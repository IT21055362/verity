Litreature Review

The rapid advancement of deepfake technology, particularly through AI and generative adversarial networks (GANs), has sparked significant concerns across society. Deepfake images—synthetically created or manipulated visual content—can replicate realistic patterns, textures, and features, making them nearly indistinguishable from real photographs. While these technologies have positive applications in entertainment and education, they also present serious risks, especially when used to spread misinformation, non-consensual explicit content, or create fake events, impacting public opinion and privacy rights.


The review highlights various deepfake detection methods—deep learning-based, physical, and signal-level approaches—and addresses the ongoing need for generalizable and real-time solutions. Despite advancements, challenges like generalizability and the evolving sophistication of deepfakes underscore the need for further research in this field.

Social bot detection remains a critical research area as bots become increasingly sophisticated on Notable advances include Kumar et al.'s [3] ensemble model combining TextCNN and LSTM with BERT embeddings, achieving 97% accuracy using only tweet content. Wei and Nguyen [4] developed BOTLE using BiLGRU and linguistic embeddings, while Martín-Gutiérrez et al. [5] created Bot-DenseNet, combining metadata with text features using BERT and RoBERTa models.

Litreature Review

Social media platforms like Twitter and Facebook have transformed news consumption, yet they also enable widespread political misinformation, threatening democratic stability and cohesion. Numerous NLP-based studies address this issue, emphasizing text preprocessing to distinguish truth from falsehood. Techniques tested by Singh and Patidar show sequential models, like LSTM, often outperform non-sequential methods in fake news detection. Recent advancements include CNN-RNN hybrids, achieving over 80% accuracy, while Majumdar et al. demonstrated LSTM's high performance in multi-class classification.


Matheven and Kumar’s LSTM-Word2Vec model showed over 94% accuracy with diverse datasets, underscoring data diversity’s impact on generalization. Similarly, Gowthami et al. reviewed high-performing models, highlighting Bi-LSTM and C-LSTM’s strength in complex pattern recognition. Mollah et al. advanced detection further by integrating RoBERTa embeddings with custom deep networks, achieving nearly 99.8% accuracy on the WELFake dataset. Bhutani et al. also found sentiment analysis a beneficial feature, revealing diverse algorithmic strengths across datasets.

Litreature Review

Clickbait detection has evolved from rule-based and traditional machine learning techniques, which relied heavily on manually crafted features, to advanced deep learning methods. Early approaches, such as those by Anand et al. [1] and Chakraborty et al. [2], used decision trees and SVMs, though these struggled to generalize across domains. With deep learning advancements, models like RNNs and CNN-LSTM combinations proposed by Razaque et al. [3] and Naeem et al. [4] have shown improved ability to capture text patterns, but they require large labeled datasets, which are often scarce in rapidly evolving online forums.

Semi-supervised learning techniques, such as those by Dai and Le [5] and Miyato et al. [6], aim to address labeled data scarcity by leveraging both labeled and unlabeled data. However, applying these methods to clickbait detection in forums remains largely unexplored. Recently, multimodal approaches, such as Bhakliwal & Aralikatte’s ensemble model [7] and Zheng et al.’s multimodal transformer [8], have incorporated visual cues but still face challenges due to limited multimodal data.

Research Problem and Solution
Research Problem

How accurately can we identify different types of misinformation (deepfakes, bots, false political content, clickbait) on social media platforms through an integrated system utilizing machine learning?



Solution

Design a multi-functional browser extension that uses machine learning to detect deepfake images, bots, political misinformation, and clickbait, providing users with real-time verification and context for social media content.

Research Objectives

Develop an integrated system for detecting and analyzing various types of misinformation on social media platforms using machine learning approaches.

System Architecture

Develop a unified system architecture that integrates deepfakes, bots, false political content, clickbait components for a comprehensive solution.

Performance Optimization

Optimize system performance to achieve high accuracy with low latency, ensuring real-time detection and analysis of misinformation.

Dataset Development

Create standardized datasets for training machine learning models to effectively identify different types of misinformation.

Methodology

The project followed a structured, iterative approach, beginning with the development of individual machine learning components for detecting deepfake images, social bots, political misinformation, and clickbait. Each module was trained, validated, and refined before being integrated into a unified browser extension using a FastAPI backend and Chrome Extension scripts to enable seamless operation. After integration, the system underwent thorough testing at both component and end-to-end levels to ensure functionality and accuracy. Finally, the extension was packaged for deployment, with comprehensive documentation provided to support user interaction and maintenance.
