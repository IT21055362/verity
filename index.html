Litreature Review

Social bot detection, a crucial area of research, refers to the identification and analysis of these bots on social media platforms. As social bots continue to evolve, they are becoming increasingly difficult to detect. Detecting social bots is crucial for maintaining the integrity and reliability of social media platforms. Due to the evolving nature of these bots, social bot detection remains a widely researched area.

Recent advancements have seen a shift towards machine learning (ML) and deep learning (DL) techniques. A systematic review [3] highlighted that DL methods, particularly those utilizing convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and recurrent neural networks (RNNs), have shown significant promise in distinguishing between human and bot accounts due to their ability to learn complex patterns in data. However, the authors also identify gaps and challenges in current DL approaches, such as the need for better feature representation and handling the multilingual nature of social media content.

Another systematic review on social media bot detection methods, [4] highlighted that machine learning techniques, especially supervised learning, emerged as the predominant approach. Features used for detection can be broadly categorized into profile information, post content, posting behaviour, and network structure. While graph-based and crowdsourcing approaches offer unique insights, they face scalability issues. Unsupervised learning shows promise in identifying coordinated bot activities. Notably, there is an increasing focus on content-based features, especially those leveraging natural language processing, as these can capture subtle linguistic patterns characteristic of bot behaviour. This multi-faceted approach reflects the complex and evolving nature of social media bots, necessitating diverse and sophisticated detection strategies.

For instance, Kumar et al.[5] proposed a novel approach for detecting Twitter bots using only tweet content. They developed an ensemble model combining TextCNN (a convolutional neural network for text classification) and LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory) with BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers) embeddings, achieving 97% accuracy and outperforming previous methods. To address class imbalance, they introduced a Language Model Oversampling Technique (LMOTE) that generated more semantically meaningful synthetic bot tweets compared to traditional methods. The ensemble architecture effectively captured both n-gram relationships and sequential patterns in the text. Overall, the study demonstrated that advanced NLP techniques and data augmentation can significantly improve automated social media bot detection using textual features alone, without relying on account metadata or network information.

Similarly, Wei and Nguyen [6] introduced BOTLE, a novel Twitter bot detection model utilizing bidirectional lightweight gated recurrent units (BiLGRU) and linguistic embeddings. This approach achieves comparable or superior performance to state-of- the-art models without relying on handcrafted features or prior user information. The study highlights the potential of text-based bot detection methods in social media analysis. However, the model's reliance solely on tweet content may limit its ability to detect sophisticated bots that mimic human language patterns. Additionally, the study does not address the model's adaptability to evolving bot behaviours or its performance on more recent datasets, which could be potential limitations in real-world applications.

Furthermore, Martín-Gutiérrez et al. [7] introduced a multilingual approach for detecting Twitter bots using transformers and deep learning. The researchers developed Bot-DenseNet, a model that combined metadata features with text-based features encoded using language models like BERT and RoBERTa, addressing language constraints of previous methods. Their model achieved a high F1-score of 0.9715 using RoBERTa embeddings, outperforming other input representations tested. The researchers faced with data imbalance, requiring careful handling of their dataset which contained more human than bot accounts. The evolving nature of bot behaviour posed another significant challenge, necessitating a model that could generalize well to potentially unseen tactics. Balancing feature selection between metadata and text-based features, while also managing model complexity and avoiding overfitting, were crucial considerations.

The detection of Twitter bots in specific contexts, such as the Sri Lankan political landscape, further highlights the importance of adaptable bot detection systems. As highlighted by the study of the 2015 presidential election, [8] bots like Siripalabot inadvertently amplified alternative voices in a media environment where government control was a concern. This created a new mediated space for democratic dialogue, particularly important in a country with growing internet and social media use.

In conclusion, the above studies demonstrate significant advancements in social bot detection, particularly in leveraging deep learning techniques and linguistic features. However, challenges remain in adapting to evolving bot behaviours, handling multilingual content, and balancing between textual and non-textual features. Our research aims to address these gaps by developing a more comprehensive model that efficiently combines various feature types while maintaining high detection accuracy.

Social media platforms such as Twitter and Facebook have revolutionized the way people consume news and information. However, they have also become conduits for the widespread dissemination of misinformation, particularly concerning political issues. This phenomenon presents a serious challenge to democratic societies, as previous research has shown that false and misleading information on social media can significantly influence public discourse and shape public opinion. The global nature of political misinformation on social media is recognized as a major challenge, with profound societal implications, including the potential to incite violence, fuel protests, exacerbate polarization, and erode trust in democratic institutions. Therefore, addressing this issue is crucial for maintaining societal stability and cohesion. 3 detecting political misinformation using NLP techniques: A survey of the state-of-the-art has been studied in a huge body of research. NLP is all about text preprocessing: the area which helps to obtain meaningful features from your raw texts, such as word frequencies sentiment linguistic patterns topic model etc. This is important for us to separate between political fact and falsehood. This process, using Natural Language Processing (NLP) to evaluate linguistic patterns and contextual information in political content has proven effective with new algorithms that are helping make things more accurate than ever before when reportedly detecting misinformation and disinformation. Based on a study by Akanksha Singh and Sanjay Patidar [1] who tested several machine learning techniques such as decision trees, naive Bayes, support vector machines, random forests, convolutional neural networks, and recurrent neural networks, to detect fake news and disinformation on social media. In many experiments, sequential classifiers capable of capturing the recurrent nature of text data, such as long short-term memory and, outperformed non-sequential models. Regarding the linear Support Vector Machines, it has also shown high precision in the classification of the false piece of news. Data preprocessing techniques like imputing missing values with most frequent values can improve the performance of fake news detection models. Traditional methods like Naive Bayes, Support Vector Machines (SVM), and neural networks (e.g. multilayer perceptron) have exhibited significant accuracy for this task. More recently, hybrid deep learning architectures that combine convolutional neural networks (CNN) and recurrent neural networks (RNN) have emerged as a promising approach, achieving over 80% accuracy in detecting fake news on tweets. Majumdar et al [2] presents an approach based on a combination of the long short-term memory recurrent neural network model for multi-class fake news classification, using a dataset that is classified as False, Partial False, True, or Other. The authors explain that while previous work has investigated machine learning and deep learning techniques, such as the CSI method or feature-based classification, none of these have achieved a high training accuracy, at most 0.98, as the LSTM model. 4 Another study by Anand Matheven and Burra Venkata Durga Kumar [3] presents a system for detecting fake news by combining deep learning (Long Short-Term Memory or LSTM) and natural language processing (Word2Vec) models. Several independent variables were analyzed to study their impact on the system's accuracy, including number of training cycles, data diversity, and vector size. The results showed that increasing the number of training epochs beyond an optimal point led to overfitting and stagnation in validation accuracy. Data diversity, in terms of the subject matter and sources of the news articles in the training data, had a significant effect on the system's generalization ability when tested on a separate dataset. Vector size also influenced accuracy, with larger vector sizes negatively impacting performance, especially when training data was limited. Overall, the proposed system achieved high accuracy of over 94% when trained on a diverse dataset and tested on an independent test set, highlighting the efficacy of combining LSTM and Word2Vec for automated fake news detection. However, the system performed poorly (63% accuracy) when trained on a narrowly focused dataset lacking diversity, underscoring the importance of representative and varied training data. A comprehensive review of machine learning models adopted for fake news detection by Gowthami et al [4] reports the performance of the best models in terms of accuracy, F1- score, precision and recall. The traditional algorithms included Random Forest, Naive Bayes, Decision Trees, SVM (Support Vector Machines), and Logistic Regression. Deep learning techniques also provided intelligent insights. They included CNN and LSTM such as, Bi-LSTM, C-LSTM Hierarchical Attention Networks, or their hybrid model CNN-LSTM. Among these, the Bi-LSTM and C-LSTM models achieved the highest accuracy of 95% on a combined corpus for fake news detection. The paper highlights the superiority of deep learning techniques, especially LSTM-based architectures, in capturing the complex patterns and linguistic nuances present in fake news data. However, the performance of models is heavily influenced by the diversity and representativeness of the training data used. 5 Mollah et al [5] presents a cutting-edge approach to fake news detection by synergistically combining the powerful RoBERTa language model with a meticulously crafted deep neural network architecture. Recognizing the limitations of previous methods, the authors leverage RoBERTa's contextual word embeddings to capture the nuanced linguistic patterns indicative of misinformation. These embeddings are then fed into a customized deep neural network designed explicitly for the fake news detection task. The network's architecture incorporates attention mechanisms, dropout layers for regularization, and dense layers for dimensionality reduction, culminating in a binary classification output. Comprehensive experiments on the well-curated WELFake dataset demonstrate the remarkable efficacy of this approach, achieving an exceptional accuracy of 99.76% and outperforming prior state-of-the-art models by a substantial margin. The authors attribute this outstanding performance to the synergy between RoBERTa's language representation capabilities and the tailored deep learning architecture, underscoring the potential of advanced language models and neural networks in combating the pernicious spread of fake news. How does it work?

  • Download the Verity Extension

    Download the Verity extension from our website or your browser's extension store. With a quick setup, you're ready to monitor your Twitter feed for deepfakes, social bots, and political misinformation in real time.
